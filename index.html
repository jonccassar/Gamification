1. What is Gamification ?

Gamifcation may be defined as the use of game design elements into a non-gaming environment. With the scope of motivating people, the Gamification concept, applies gaming elements like trophies, badges, points, avatars and leader boards to a non-gaming context such as place of work. Gamification can also be expressed as the utilization of game mechanics, dynamics and framework for the promotion of certain desired audience behaviour. More specifically, Gamification can be termed as the utilization of mechanics, feedback loops, loyalty, fun and rewards that server as interaction boosters.

3. Project Summary

3.1 Research Question

Can the writing of a dissertation be made more enjoyable and engaging through gamifcation to the extent of making students want to undertake the same process again ?

3.2 Introduction

The objective of this study is to employ Gamifcation on a subset of the academic (research) with the attempt to make the undertaking more engaging. In view of this, this study, focused on the dissertation writing process among undergraduate students. Thus, effort was made to gamify the process and to make the process engage to the extent of making students want to undertake the same process again. This was done through the development of an Android mobile application. The application served as a data collection prototype and the collected data aided in the conduct of hypotheses testing. Results from the hypotheses were discussed to form arguments in favour or against Gamifcation. The following sections describe how these aims and objectives were materialise.

3.3 Literature

The study kick started by reviewing an array of literature filed by game designers, practitioners, academics, game developers and psychologists. Here, Deterding's Gamification definition was examined where the term was unpacked into 4 segments: Game, Element, Design, and Non-gaming context and explained independently.

After relaxing the Gamification definition, this study reviewed the science and psychology behind Gamification with special emphasis to the FBM model. In conjunction with the FBM Model, The Flow and Bartle's Player Model were also discussed. From this point onwards, literature pertaining to game mechanics, design and aesthetics was reviewed. Here, arguments related to the MDA Framework, points, levels, leader boards, badges, challenges and on boarding, Gamifcation 1.0 and Smart Gamification.

The proceeding step in literature looked at existing systems that are close to this dissertation. System like Write or Die, Epic Win, 750 Words and Pain Squad App were reviewed and documented. The final entry in Chapter 2 reviews criticism filed against Gamification with arguments stating that the term is misleading or that Gamification activities put users at the risk of getting wrapped up in the reward loop forever.

3.4 Methodology

The reviewed literature was used to design a methodology with which the problem statement could be studied. Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics were briefly reviewed and utilised to build up UML diagrams, mockups designs and a paper prototype. The paper prototype was tested through a pilot study composed of 10 subjects.

Feedback from the prototyping was noted and incorporated in the application main design. The Chapter proceeded in describing briefly the MoSCoW method and how such method was used to prioritise the prototype's features. Chapter 4 progressed by listing the prototype's main features with special emphasis on earning of points from the conduct of activities, notifications and schedules. Furthermore, the prototype's mechanics and their implementation through the utilisation of Views, Permissions, Layouts and other Android's API components were discussed. The next step in Chapter 4 provided a brief account on testing, deployment, availability of resources and assumption drawn during the development of the prototype.

As the prototype served as a data collection tool, Chapter 4 outlines the data collection methodologies. A pool of 75 full-time students who were currently in the process of writing a dissertation were randomly chosen from University of Malta(UoM) campus. Subjects were encouraged to test the prototype and to answer to a set of questions. After a close review of the problem statement, the null and alternative hypotheses were formed upon which the Chi-Square test was ran. The Comparing Probabilities test was used to check the strength of the results

3.5 Results

Results section reports the key findings from the employment of the methods discussed in the previous section. Discussed firstly is the meta-data with emphasis on the survey mode, data collection and response. Following, is discussed the general descriptive statistics which aimed to highlight interesting distributions according to gender. Results are presented in two-way contingency tables showing both observed and expected data.

The second part discussed the inferential statistics where reference to the null, alternative hypotheses, Goodness of Fit test and Comparing Probabilities was made. Here the p-values and the Chi-Square values obtained from the statistical tests were recorded and analysed. This lead to the discussion of the rejection of the null hypothesis on the bases of the p-values and the Chi-Square values obtained.

The last section made use of the Comparing Probabilities tests with the attempt to study the strength of the obtained significance. The inferred results provided evidence that the level of significance achieved is not due to chance but because strong ties exist between the research variables. This provided the basis for arguments in favour of Gamification and the research process which were discussed in the next section.

3.6 Conclusion

In final, Chapter 5 discussed interesting results obtained from the gathering of data. These findings were compared to either similar findings reported by academics or statistical data compiled by Governmental institutions. This section reported that Gamification (implemented as an Android mobile application) succeeded in instigating engagement among the sampled pool. This was formally noted through the collected data and informally through the exhilarated behaviour shown by subjects during the hands on session.

Motivation encouraged more students to be willing to undertake the same process again, thus continue their studies beyond a first degree. Engagement and further more task repetition rely heavily on the type of rewards used and whether users are motivated by such rewards. In Chapter 5 this argument lead to the intrinsic and extrinsic debate and which, among the two is best to use in a gamified application. The argument advanced by stating that a mixture of both is the optimal solution and that this could be included as a potential future project.

Other arguments mentioned in Chapter 5 investigated Gamification from the production perspective. Here, it was mentioned that in general, Gamification can serve as a production booster and that Gamification can indeed offer a solution for frustration, procrastination and time management. Other arguments raised dealt with Gamification as a potential tool for the institution in particular for Universities. This argument along with others set the basis for potential future work that could pick up from where this study ended.

4. Application Features

4.1 Daily Notifications
  • Send notifications every 6 hours - starting at 8.00am
  • LED light flashes 4 times in Magenta colour
  • Mobile vibration
  • Mobile ring tone
4.2 Schedule
  • Connects to gmail calendar and show daily events
  • Indicates total amount of free time less 8 hours of sleep
  • Encourages users to work on the dissertation during fee time
4.3 Points, Badges and Levels

Points can be earned by conducting actions related to the production of a dissertation. The application supports three activities from which, points can be earned and these are;

  • Reading Papers (15 points per each read)
  • Writing dissertation chapters (35%, 80%, 100% completed earn 10, 40 and 50 points)
  • Conduct dissertation surveys (2 points per each survey completed)
    • In the process users can progress through six levels through which badges can be earned.
