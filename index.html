1. What is Gamification ?

Gamifcation may be defined as the use of game design elements into a non-gaming environment. With the scope of motivating people, the gamification concept, applies gaming elements like trophies, badges, points, avatars and leader boards to a non-gaming context such as place of work. Gamification can also be expressed as the utilization of game mechanics, dynamics and framework for the promotion of certain desired audience behaviour. More specifically, gamification can be termed as the utilization of mechanics, feedback loops, loyalty, fun and rewards that server as interaction boosters.

3. Project Summary

3.1 Research Question

The purpose of this study is to analsye whether gamification cause the dissertation process to become more engaging to the extent of making students want to undertake the same process again.

3.2 Introduction
3.3 Literature

The study kick start by reviewing an array of literature filed by game designers, practitioners, academics, game developers and psychologists. Here Deterding's gamification definition was examined where the term was unpacked into 4 segments namely Game, Element, Design, and Non-gaming context and explained independently.

After relaxing the definition, this study reviewed the science and psychology behind gamification with special emphasis to the FBM model. In conjunction with the FBM model, The Flow and Bartle's Player model were also discussed. From this point onwards, literature pertaining to game mechanics, design and aesthetics was reviewed. Here, arguments related to the MDA framework, points, levels, leader boards, badges, challenges and on boarding, Gamifcation 1.0 and smart gamification.

The proceeding step in literature looked at existing systems that are close to this dissertation. System like Write or Die, Epic Win, 750 Words and Pain Squad App were reviewed and documented. The final entry in Chapter 2 reviews criticism filed against gamification with arguments stating that the term is misleading or that gamification activities put users at the risk of getting wrapped up in the pointing system.

3.4 Methodology

The Reviewed literature was used to design a methodology with which the problem statement could be studied. Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics were briefly reviewed and utilised to build up UML diagrams, mockups designs paper prototyping. The paper prototype was tested through a pilot study composed of 10 individuals.

Feedback from the prototyping was noted and incorporated in the designs. The chapter proceeded in describing briefly the MoSCoW method and how such method was used to prioritise the prototype's features. Chapter 4 progressed by listing the prototype's main features with special emphasis on earning of points from the conduct of activities, notifications and schedules. Furthermore, the prototype's mechanics and their implementation through the utilisation of views, permissions, layouts and other Android's API components was discussed. The next step in chapter 4 provides a brief account on testing, deployment, availability of resources and assumption drawn during the development of the prototype.

As the prototype serves as a data collection tool, Chapter 4 outlines the data collection methodologies. A pool of 75 full-time students who were currently in the process of writing a dissertation were randomly chosen from UoM campus. Subjects were encouraged to test the prototype and to answer to a set of questions. After a close review of the problem statement, the null and alternative hypothesis were formed upon which the chi-square test was ran. The comparing probabilities test was used to check the strength of the results

4. Application Features

4.1 Daily Notifications
  • Send notifications every 6 hours - starting at 8.00am
  • LED light flashes 4 times in Magenta colour
  • Mobile vibration
  • Mobile ring tone
4.2 Schedule
  • Connects to gmail calendar and show daily events
  • Indicates total amount of free time less 8 hours of sleep
  • Encourages users to work on the dissertation during fee time
4.3 Points, Badges and Levels

Points can be earned by conducting actions related to the production of a dissertation. The application supports three activities from which, points can be earned and these are;

  • Reading Papers (15 points per each read)
  • Writing dissertation chapters (35%, 80%, 100% completed earn 10, 40 and 50 points)
  • Conduct dissertation surveys (5 points per each survey completed)
    • In the process users can progress through six levels through which badges can be earned.
